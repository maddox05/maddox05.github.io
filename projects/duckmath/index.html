The Story


DuckMath started in October 2021 when I was 16 years old, a junior at Brebeuf Jesuit Preparatory School in Indianapolis. I saw someone younger than me on TikTok who had built an unblocked games site and thought "I can do that." The thing is, I had barely coded before. But I started anyway.


What began as a simple unblocked games site turned into a platform hosting 250+ browser-based games across categories like Multiplayer, Flash, Emulator, Platformer, Puzzle, Sports, and more. The whole point was simple: give students a way to play games at school when everything else was blocked.


I worked on it 5-30 minutes a day for years. No grand plan, no investors, no team. Just me, iterating constantly, learning by doing. As I like to say: "I learned through doing mostly."

The $240 Day


For three years DuckMath grew slowly. I'd tweak the design, add games, fix things that broke. Then in December 2024, the site earned $240 in a single day. That was the moment everything changed. I was working an internship at the time and I realized this little side project had more potential than I'd ever given it credit for. I left the internship and went all-in.

The Marketing Machine


Growth didn't happen by accident. I built a content machine: 12 short-form videos per day across TikTok, YouTube Shorts, Instagram Reels, and Snapchat. I used an auto-upload platform to distribute content simultaneously across all platforms. The formula was simple - videos like "how to play X game in school" with DuckMath as the answer.


I constantly analyzed what content hit and iterated on the formula. The result? In August 2025, the site went from 5,000 to 150,000 daily active users in just two weeks.


The Journey In Screenshots



From 1,828 views on my first ever TikTok to 1.7 million views on a single video. SEO was massive too - Google Search Console showed 1.34 million clicks, 3.18 million impressions, and a 42.2% click-through rate.

Tech Stack


DuckMath was intentionally simple. The entire site was HTML, CSS, and JavaScript hosted on GitHub Pages. Games were delivered as browser-based HTML5, Flash emulation, and WebGL. I owned 20+ domains as backups so if one got blocked by a school, students could just go to another. The site also had cloaking tools and proxy integration to bypass filters.


On top of the games, I built a leaderboard system, an in-site economy with coins, a seasonal battle pass, and a Discord proxy. Revenue came from ads - minimal and non-intrusive, but at 150k daily users it added up fast.


The kicker? A full rebuild of the entire site would take me 3-4 days. Simple tech, massive distribution.

The Exit


In November 2025, FreezeNova - a global gaming company that had previously acquired similar sites - reached out. They offered $100K. I countered with $120K. They agreed.


At the time DuckMath was pulling $15,000-$20,000 per month in ad revenue. Selling wasn't about the money running out - it was about wanting to move on. The unblocked games space wasn't where I wanted to build long-term. I had bigger ideas.


The sale was covered by the Purdue Exponent, Inside Indiana Business, Fox 4 KC, WLFI, and YouAreCurrent. Not bad for something I started as a 16-year-old who could barely code.

What I Learned


DuckMath taught me that distribution beats product. The tech was simple. What made it work was relentless marketing, understanding my audience (students on school Chromebooks), and solving a real problem they had every day.


It also taught me that consistency compounds. Three years of 5-30 minutes a day doesn't feel like much in the moment, but it adds up to something real. When the viral moment came, I was ready because I'd spent years quietly building the foundation.
