The Story


After selling DuckMath for $120K, I knew the next thing had to be bigger. DuckMath taught me that students on school Chromebooks are an underserved audience with real demand. The problem was that browser games only go so far - students wanted to play real games like Roblox, Clash Royale, and Toca Boca, but those require native apps that school Chromebooks can't install.


The solution: stream the games from the cloud. Run Android games on remote servers and pipe the video to any browser. No downloads, no installs, no hardware requirements. That's MaddoxCloud.


I built it in about a month of 10-hour days and officially launched in December 2025.

How It Works


MaddoxCloud runs Android emulator instances on remote servers. When a user picks a game, the server launches an Android instance with that game, captures the video and audio output, and streams it to the user's browser in real-time via WebRTC. The user's keyboard, mouse, and touch inputs get relayed back to the server, so it feels like the game is running locally.


The result: you can play Roblox, Clash Royale, or any Android game on a school Chromebook, a crappy laptop, or even a phone browser. All you need is an internet connection.

Tech Stack


The infrastructure behind MaddoxCloud is the most complex thing I've built. Here's what powers it:


  • Streaming: WebRTC for low-latency video/audio streaming from server to browser and input relay back
  • Android Emulation: Server-side Android emulator instances running game APKs on cloud compute
  • CDN/Security: Cloudflare for DDoS protection, caching, and global distribution
  • Frontend: Web application served through the browser
  • SSL: Google Trust Services, domain validated

The hardest part isn't the code - it's the infrastructure scaling. Each user needs their own Android instance running on a server, which means compute costs scale linearly with users. That's the challenge I'm working on solving right now.

The Business Model


MaddoxCloud runs on a freemium model. Free users get 10-minute play sessions and wait in a queue when servers are at capacity. Paid users get unlimited play time with no waitlist.


The waitlist has been both a blessing and a curse. At peak times, queues hit 12,000+ people. That's incredible demand validation, but it also means users get frustrated waiting. The long-term plan is to make the platform fully free once I figure out the infrastructure scaling and can bring costs down enough.

Marketing


I applied the same playbook from DuckMath: aggressive short-form video across every platform. TikTok, Snapchat, YouTube Shorts, Instagram Reels. The content targets the same audience - students who want to play games on their school Chromebooks.


One Snapchat post - "Schools tried to ban maddoxcloud.com so I did 15 jumping jacks, destroyed their firewall, and now I'm back in" - pulled 357 likes and showed the kind of viral, relatable content that resonates with students. When schools block the domain, that just becomes more content.

What's Next


MaddoxCloud is still early. Two months in with 50K users and $500 MRR is solid, but the real challenge is scaling the infrastructure to handle demand without burning through cash. I'm working on:


  • Reducing server costs per user session
  • Improving stream quality and reducing latency
  • Expanding the game library
  • Eventually making the entire platform free

DuckMath proved I could build something millions of people use. MaddoxCloud is the next step - harder tech, harder scaling, but a much bigger opportunity.
